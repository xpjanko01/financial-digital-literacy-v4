About the project

Digital finance is now the default—payments, investing apps, online lending, and platform-based services. This shift creates new opportunities, but also new vulnerabilities: scams, dark patterns, pseudo-advice, and misinformation that can amplify risky behavior and financial loss.

Our project addresses these challenges by combining economic and behavioral perspectives with data-driven methods. We aim to deliver tools that are useful not only for researchers, but also for educators, institutions, and policy makers.

Main objectives

  • Develop a comparable definition of financial digital literacy for the V4 context.
  • Design a measurement approach (index + questionnaire) capturing skills and exposure to digital risks.
  • Create educational modules and guidance for universities, educators, and the public.
  • Provide a scalable web tool enabling evidence-based interventions and monitoring.

Why it matters (economic angle)

  • Information frictions increase transaction and screening costs in digital financial ecosystems.
  • Misinformation can distort financial choices and household welfare, especially online.
  • Better literacy supports resilience, reduces harm, and strengthens trust in the ecosystem.

Work packages (draft structure)

WP1 — Concept & Framework

Define financial digital literacy for the V4 and map key risk channels in platform-based finance.

WP2 — Measurement & Index

Design and validate a cross-country comparable index and survey modules.

WP3 — Data & Analytics

Use modern analytics to understand risk exposure and decision patterns in digital finance.

WP4 — Education Toolkit

Create practical modules, case studies, and exercises for higher education and public learning.

WP5 — Digital Platform

Implement a web-based interface for learning, self-assessment, and dissemination.

WP6 — Dissemination & Policy

Translate results into actionable recommendations for institutions and policy stakeholders.

Planned outputs

  • Financial Digital Literacy Index (V4-ready) + methodology.
  • Survey instrument and documentation for replication.
  • Educational toolkit (modules, case studies, exercises).
  • Online platform for self-assessment and learning.
  • Academic papers and practitioner-oriented briefs.

Impact

  • Better identification of vulnerable groups and risk drivers in digital finance.
  • Evidence-based improvements in education and training content.
  • Stronger resilience against scams, manipulation, and misinformation.
  • Higher trust and more efficient decision-making within financial ecosystems.
